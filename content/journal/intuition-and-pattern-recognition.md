"Trust your intuition" is good advice given to people who can't yet tell their intuition apart from their conditioning. For them — for most of us, often — it's nearly useless, because the loudest inner voice is rarely the wisest one. It's usually just the oldest.

So here's a distinction I've found more useful than the word *intuition* on its own.

## Two kinds of knowing

**Pattern recognition** is your nervous system matching the present against the past. It's fast, it's protective, and it's frequently right — but it's right about a world that may no longer exist. It says: *this feels like the last time, so it will end like the last time.* That's not intuition. That's memory with a deadline.

**Intuition**, in the sense worth trusting, is quieter. It tends to arrive without urgency and without a story attached. It rarely flatters you. It often asks for something inconvenient and offers no argument for why.

> Fear is loud and fluent. Intuition is quiet and a little embarrassing, because it usually points at the thing you were hoping not to have to do.

## A field test

When a signal arrives, I find it helps to ask three plain questions.

1. **Does it have a story?** Pattern recognition comes with a narrative — a case, a precedent, a list of evidence. Intuition usually comes bare.
2. **Does it escalate when I attend to it?** Fear gets louder the longer you look. A true signal tends to hold steady, or soften, under attention.
3. **Whose voice is it in?** If you can hear a specific person — a parent, an ex, a former version of yourself — you've probably found conditioning, not knowing.

None of this is a formula. It's a way of slowing the moment down enough that you can tell who, exactly, is doing the talking.

## Why it matters

People in transition make their worst decisions when they mistake panic for guidance. They also make them when they override genuine knowing because it isn't loud enough to feel official.

The point isn't to stop feeling things strongly. It's to stop outsourcing your life to the strongest feeling in the room — especially when that feeling is just the past, insisting that nothing has changed.
