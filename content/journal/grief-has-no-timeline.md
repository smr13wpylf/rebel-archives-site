A short one this week, because some subjects don't want to be tidy.

Grief does not move in the direction we're told it should. We're handed a vocabulary of stages and timelines — as if loss were a project with milestones — and then we feel like we're failing when the project refuses to close.

It refuses because it was never a project.

> Grief isn't a problem you're solving. It's a relationship you're continuing, in a new form, with someone or something that is no longer here to meet you halfway.

The arithmetic is strange. Some losses get lighter and then, on an ordinary Tuesday with no anniversary attached, arrive with full force, as if no time had passed at all. Other losses sit quietly for years and then resolve almost overnight, for reasons you can't reconstruct. The body keeps a calendar the mind can't read.

What I've come to think is that the goal was never to get over it. The goal — if there is one — is to widen enough to carry it without being defined by it. Not smaller grief. A larger life around the same grief.

That's slower than closure and less satisfying to talk about. But it has the advantage of being true, and truth, in this territory, is the only thing that eventually offers any rest.
