There's a version of change that looks like courage and is actually flight. It moves fast, burns clean, and leaves a satisfying trail of smoke. From the outside it can be indistinguishable from reinvention. From the inside, eventually, it isn't — because escape always asks you to leave the same thing again, somewhere new.

## The tell

Escape is allergic to the present. It needs *elsewhere*. A different city, a different body, a different name for the same loneliness. The energy is centrifugal — everything flung outward, away from a center that never gets examined.

Reinvention can involve all the same moves — the move, the ending, the new name — but the energy runs the other direction. It's centripetal. It's a return toward something, not just a sprint away from something.

> You can tell the two apart by what they refuse to carry. Escape refuses to carry the self. Reinvention insists on it.

## Why escape is so tempting

Because it works, briefly. There's real relief in a clean break, and the relief is genuine enough to be confused with arrival. For a few months, the new arrangement holds. The old patterns are quiet because they haven't had time to follow you yet.

They always follow you. The pattern was never in the city or the job or the relationship. It was in the way you meet things. Geography doesn't touch that.

## What reinvention actually requires

- **A reckoning, not just a departure.** Some honest accounting of what you're leaving and why — including your own part in it.
- **A center you're willing to keep.** Not everything about the old life was the problem. Escape can't tell the difference; reinvention has to.
- **Tolerance for the in-between.** The hardest part isn't the leaving or the arriving. It's the corridor between them, where you're no longer the old thing and not yet the new one.

Most people can't bear the corridor, so they keep moving — and movement, mistaken for progress, becomes a way of never arriving anywhere at all.

The quieter, harder path is to stop running long enough to find out what you'd actually choose, and then to choose it on purpose. That's not escape. That's the beginning of a life you don't need to flee.
